Ode to the metabolists

Allow me a brief design-nerd digression. I've long been a fan of the Japanese Metabolist movement, an architectural philosophy that fuses together principles of organic growth and scalable urban development. The idea is that you can add and remove sections, allowing the buildings to evolve with the needs of their inhabitants. Think repetitively modular components and organic shapes, fused together to form futuristic, celluluar-like structures.


The most famous example of this is the Nakagin Capsule Tower in Tokyo (in the US, there's also Marina City in Chicago).

Despite being so multi-functional, these buildings can be broken down to a few simple, repeating shapes. It's the scaleability of systems design at its most fundamental, applied to architecture.


I wanted these illustrations to feel similar: like complex compositions comprised of simple, basic shapes. I created a set of starting elements — building blocks, if you will — that combined

These modules could then be combined in a myriad different ways, Because the project was on a fairly tight deadline, taking this scalable approach also allowed for more efficient execution. As in Metabolistic architecture, components could be layered and built on top of each other, combined in a myriad of visually diverse ways.
